Unit 9 | IA & Navigation

IA & Navigation Prototype.

This week, you’re an Information Architect who wants to understand how people navigate a government agency website so that you can develop a more user-centered user interface (UI) system.

Objectives

Last week, you applied the research phase of the design thinking process. This week, you’ll be working in the definition phase of the design thinking process.
You’ll be creating a new IA for your government agency by analyzing the UI navigation, card sorting, redesigning the sitemap, and creating a new UI navigation prototype.

Today’s homework is the continuation of the government agency design challenge. Much of the assignment you have already begun working on during in-class activities.

Analyzing the navigation of a website from a heuristic perspective.

Running usability tests on the navigation of a website.

Performing card sorting

Redesigning a new sitemap based on card sorting results

Prototyping a new navigation UI based on a sitemap

Deliverables

1. ⟶ Heuristic Evaluation of Navigation
+ MARK AS DONE

‣ Pair up and analyze the navigation of your website with usability heuristics in mind.


‣ Take screenshots of your agency's navigation structures (top nav, side nav, footer, breadcrumbs, tabs, etc.) and begin redlining/annotating these screenshots by pointing out problems, as well as highlighting anything that you believe is already working quite well.


‣ Finally, suggest solutions to these problems and briefly explain why you think these will improve navigation.

Take advantage of the previous heuristic analysis templates you've used in class, these are great cheat-sheets for recalling many of the most common problems you'll see in UI design. Better yet, start building out your own list of 'usual suspects' for use in future exercises too

If you're struggling to identify problems or solutions, reflect on the research you have already done and try to frame your approach via "I wish..." statements "I wish it was easier to find local news...", "I wish it didn't take so many clicks to get here...", etc, and imagine how you would make that wish come true - there's your proposal

2. ⟶ Navigation Usability Testing
+ MARK AS DONE

‣ Pair up and conduct 2 to 4 usability tests specifically for the navigation components you previously identified


‣ For these tests, at least one must test the desktop version and at least one must test the mobile version


‣ Make notes of your findings, with a focus on identifying key pain points


Remember - you are the expert. It's up to you to decide how and what you test. If you're feeling stuck, look at the problems you already identified. Could you get test subjects to verify that these are actually problems? If they are, maybe you can press them for their preferences? This is how you find insights early on.

Everyone has their own style of testing, so don't think about what templates to use or what class activity to refer to, simply state an objective and test for it. ie, "I believe it's hard to find specific things on the ATO website. Can I confirm this by seeing if a user demonstrates the same paint point? If I propose a different flow, will they tell me that's a great idea or suggest something else?"

3. ⟶ Create New Sitemap
+ MARK AS DONE

‣ Work independently to create a new sitemap based on your card sorting


‣ Ensure your sitemap encompasses all major navigation elements, including top navs, side navs and footers


Use any method you want to point out where certain elements in your sitemap will manifest. ie, add a legend with unique icons for 'top nav', 'side nav' and 'footer' and apply those icons to the relevant entries in your sitemap.

Alternatively, draw large frames or 'swimlanes' around different tiers of your sitemap to point out where that level will appear in your UI.

4. ⟶ Prototype New Navigation Components
+ MARK AS DONE

‣ Based on your sitemap, work independently to prototype a new primary navigation component that incorporates your proposed solutions and new IA. (Just the primary nav is enough for now, which usually manifests as a top nav)


‣ Start with either a desktop or mobile prototype and demonstrate any additional states as necessary - ie dropdowns, accordions. Take this to a low to mid fidelity finish


BONUS: Produce a responsive primary nav component prototype. If you started with mobile, now make the desktop version. If you started with desktop - make it work for mobile. Also consider producing secondary and tertiary nav prototypes such as side panels and footers


You don't need any page templates ready to start making a clickable nav prototype. Simply link all your nav links to blank or placeholder screens.

Low to mid fidelity means colour is allowed, and if time permits you can begin considering the cosmetic quality (how good it looks and feels) of your design.
